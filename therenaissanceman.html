The Renaissance Man

A Quest for Modern Polymathy

In 1721, an edition of the Boston News-Letter included the phrase, “Jack of all trades and it would seem, good at none.” This is known today as the first recorded instance of this phrase in any American publication. Through centuries of slight modifications, it has transformed into the now widely known figure of speech, “A Jack of all trades is a master of none.” Despite the popularity of this quote in this particular phrasing, this is merely an American adaptation of an even older saying. The original quote from Geffray Mynshul’s 1618 book “Essays and Characters of a Prison” is both analogous to the modern version and simultaneously completely opposite in meaning. “Jack of all trades, master of none, though oftentimes better than a master of one.” The modern interpretation of the quote is understood as taking a disapproving stance on the acquisition of skill in multiple disciplines, with the implication that such an endeavor leads to a lack of true depth in any of the fields explored. The original quote was intended to mean quite the opposite, that the journey to skill in many activities of knowledge in many disciplines is oftentimes more rewarding and notable than the search for mastery in one specific field. 

The ideas explored through the original quote can be accurately articulated through the study of polymathy, originally defined by Johann Von Wowern in his book “De Polymathia Tractacio” as “knowledge of various matters, drawn from all kinds of studies.” The general understanding of a Polymath today is a person who excels in multiple areas of study as opposed to just one, a generalist as opposed to a specialist. The psychological justification for such a goal comes from the following ideas: that the value of knowledge in the process of learning itself is often more beneficial than the actual knowledge gained on a discrete subject through the learning process. While sustained effort on one pursuit may yield better results in the short term, efforts in a wide variety of pursuits benefit broadening the mind and yield overall greater knowledge in the long term. Furthermore, that width of knowledge is often better suited to problem-solving and creative thinking than depth of knowledge. A turn to history can help to exemplify these concepts.

The most famous polymath to ever live is Leonardo da Vinci. Da Vinci is credited with both the most famous painting of all time (the “Mona Lisa”) and the most expensive painting to ever sell at public auction (the “Salvator Mundi”), but his fame extends far beyond that of the art world. He produced the first ever form of technical drawing, with over 5,000 pages filled with detailed representations of countless inventions and mechanical designs, many of which were conceived and can be accredited in part or in whole to him during and beyond his lifetime. Some of da Vinci’s additional accomplishments include the role of senior military architect and general engineer in the papal army, vast research into anatomy and physiology, advancements in early cartography, plans for a canal in Florence that were carried out to his exact specifications centuries later, and many more. In the centuries of writings on da Vinci and his works to follow, the most succinct summary of his proficiencies has been to label him as a great thinker. The core skill, the talent at the root of all of his accomplishments, was his ability to think. In his journey to study all that the world had to offer, the variety and breadth of his learning built a foundation that would support his future studies. His skill in painting was fundamental in his ability to document and express the human body. His understanding of the human body was fundamental to his understanding of the complex machines he would come to design. His understanding of these machines, their parts and proportions, and the thought process of designing them proved fundamental to the effort and design process of his painting. He could only achieve greatness in a given field through his work in seemingly disconnected areas of study, as all are connected by thinking, learning, and developing. 

It is these processes that underlie all that there is for man to accomplish. To learn, think, and develop are not only skills in their own right but serve as the building blocks for what the concept of skill even is. More and more, Western society has become geared towards specialization, and early specialization at that. Students are expected to decide career paths that will impact the rest of their professional lives while they are still children, with indecision or a change of plans hindering their success. Even by delaying the process of specialization a few years and giving students more time to explore and learn new subjects, growth factors within and enjoyment of career paths increase drastically. This demonstrates that time spent engaging in hobbies, side-projects, and fields of personal interest is never time lost. All learning, growth, and thinking in any discipline is valuable to all other disciplines, sometimes even more so than that discipline may be to itself.

In Malcolm Gladwell’s book “Outliers: The Story of Success,” he defines the 10,000-hour rule. This rule states that it takes 10,000 hours of practice to gain true expertise in any given skill. While this rule can serve as a cool fact, it is time to ditch it as a goal to strive towards. Let there instead be a new 10,000-hour rule, one that does not stress spending 10,000 hours on one subject but rather 1,000 hours on ten different subjects. Spend 500 hours on 20 different subjects. Spend 100 hours on 100 subjects. Learn a new instrument. Learn two new instruments. Start playing a new sport. Figure out how to use a new piece of software. Find some new books to read. Pick up interior decorating. Pick up sewing. Start writing. Learn how to solve a Rubik’s cube. Learn how to juggle. This rule may not lead to “true expertise” on any of the activities pursued, but maybe, just maybe, something entirely different could be achieved in the process. Maybe, through this rule, one could learn how to learn, how to think, and how to improve. Maybe one could learn something that no discipline in itself could teach, but something that is only possible through a combination of everything else. And that might be the key to true expertise itself.
